Bali: Why the Island of the Gods Never Gets Old

Bali is one of those rare destinations that captures the imagination and refuses to let go. It's an island where Hindu temples perch dramatically on cliff edges above crashing waves, where rice farmers tend centuries-old terraces in misty highland valleys, where surfers ride world-class breaks at dawn, and where the evenings glow with the warmth of ceremony and dance.

For Filipino travelers, Bali is especially accessible — just a short flight away and no visa required. It's a destination that rewards first-timers and brings back repeat visitors year after year.

The Gates of Heaven — Pura Lempuyang

No image of Bali is more recognizable than the split gates of Pura Lempuyang Luhur — known worldwide as the "Gates of Heaven." Perched at 1,175 meters above sea level on the slopes of Mount Lempuyang in eastern Bali, this ancient Hindu temple complex offers one of the most dramatic views on the island: the symmetrical candi bentar (split gate) perfectly framing Mt. Agung in the background, with its reflection appearing in a still pool below.

The temple is a place of genuine spiritual significance for Balinese Hindus and one of Bali's nine directional temples (Kahyangan Jagat). Visitors are required to wear a sarong. Arrive before 6:00 AM for the shortest queue — waits can stretch to 3–4 hours during peak season.

Ubud — Bali's Cultural Heart

Set amid rice terraces and forest, Ubud is Bali's cultural center — home to artists, healers, and some of the island's finest dining. Don't miss the Tegallalang Rice Terraces, the Sacred Monkey Forest Sanctuary, traditional Kecak dance performances at sunset, and the art galleries along Jalan Raya Ubud.

  • Tegallalang Rice Terraces — iconic terraced paddy fields with swings and cafes
  • Tirta Empul Temple — sacred spring where Balinese come for ritual purification
  • Campuhan Ridge Walk — a peaceful walk through green hills
  • Ubud Palace & Ubud Market — traditional crafts, textiles, and batik

Seminyak & Canggu — Beach Clubs & Sunsets

Bali's southwest coast is home to its most glamorous beach scene. Seminyak has upscale boutiques, rooftop bars, and beachfront sunset cocktails. Canggu has a more bohemian vibe — Instagram-worthy cafes, rice paddy views, surf lessons, and the famous beach clubs of Echo Beach and Berawa.

Tanah Lot — Temple on the Sea

One of Bali's most photographed landmarks, Tanah Lot is a temple built on a rock formation rising from the sea. At sunset, the silhouette of the temple against an orange sky is absolutely magical. Arrive 45 minutes before sunset for the best light and to secure a good viewing spot.

Visa Info for Filipinos: Filipino passport holders can enter Indonesia visa-free for up to 30 days for tourism purposes. No visa application required — just a valid passport and return ticket. The 30-day stay can be extended once at an immigration office.

Best Time to Visit

Bali's dry season runs from April to October, with July and August being the busiest months. May, June, and September offer the best balance of good weather and manageable crowds. The wet season (November to March) brings daily rain showers — usually short but intense — and lower prices.

Getting Around Bali

The most practical way to get around Bali is by hiring a private driver for the day (affordable and flexible) or using Grab (ride-hailing app). Scooter rentals are popular but carry risk on busy roads. Taxis are available at the airport and in tourist areas.

Travel Tips

  • Dress modestly when visiting temples — sarongs are often provided or rented at the entrance
  • Bargain at markets — starting price is usually double the fair price
  • Avoid single-use plastic — Bali has made sustainability efforts and many shops have gone green
  • Be respectful of ceremonies — if you encounter a procession, step aside and be quiet
  • Try local warungs (small family restaurants) for the best and cheapest Balinese food

Quick Facts

  • Best time: Apr – Oct
  • Currency: Indonesian Rupiah (IDR)
  • Language: Bahasa Indonesia, Balinese
  • Flight from Manila: ~3 hours
  • Visa-free for Filipinos (30 days)
